# Newsletter Editor — Make the Open Worth It

A newsletter lives or dies on two moments: the **subject line** (gets the open) and the **first screen** (earns the read). Everything else is craft — tight summaries, scannable structure, a consistent voice, and one clear action. Respect the reader's inbox: every item must earn its place, and the issue should be skimmable in seconds and rewarding if read in full.

## What this skill produces

**Chat-first.** Default: the drafted issue (subject line + preview text, intro, sectioned items with summaries, CTA) in markdown, or the specific piece asked for (just subject-line options, just summaries). Keep the editorial voice consistent (→ `brand-voice`).

## When to use

- "Write / edit this newsletter issue." / "Turn these links into an issue."
- "Draft a subject line." / "Summarize these for my digest."
- "What sections should my newsletter have?"
- "Make this newsletter more engaging / scannable."

## Anatomy of an issue

1. **Subject line** — the highest-leverage text. Specific and curiosity-driving over clickbait; ~30–50 chars (mobile truncates). Offer 3–5 options in different angles (benefit, curiosity, number, news). Pair with **preview/preheader text** (the snippet after the subject — don't waste it on "View in browser").
2. **Intro / hook** — 1–3 sentences that set up the issue and give a reason to keep reading. Personal/voice-driven beats generic.
3. **Body sections** — group items logically (theme, format, or a fixed recurring structure readers learn). Each item: a **clear headline**, a **2–4 sentence summary that adds your take** (not just a copied blurb), and the link. Curate ruthlessly — fewer great items beat many mediocre ones.
4. **One primary CTA** — what you want the reader to do (reply, click, share, subscribe). Don't bury it among ten links; one clear ask.
5. **Sign-off** — consistent, human.

## Writing the summaries (the real craft)

- **Add value, don't echo.** Summarize *and* say why it matters / your angle — that's why they read your newsletter instead of the source.
- **Front-load the point.** First sentence carries it; readers skim.
- **One idea per item**; cut throat-clearing ("In this fascinating article, the author explores…").
- Use `summarize` for raw source digestion, then layer your editorial voice on top.

## Scannability & format

- **Skimmable hierarchy:** bold headlines, short paragraphs (1–3 sentences), white space, bullets for lists. Most readers scan first, then dip in.
- **Mobile-first** — the majority read on phones; short lines, no wide tables, tappable links.
- **Consistent structure** issue-to-issue so regulars know where to find things.
- Keep it as long as it's good and no longer; a tight issue respects time.

## Subject lines & deliverability (briefly)

- **Subject-line angles:** benefit ("3 ways to X"), curiosity ("The mistake everyone makes"), specificity/number, timeliness, personal. A/B test if the platform allows (→ `ab-testing`).
- **Avoid spam triggers:** ALL CAPS, excessive !!!, "FREE $$$", misleading subjects (hurts deliverability and trust). Authentic from-name, consistent sending, and engaged lists land in the inbox.

## Chat output format

```
**Issue #42 — draft**

Subject options:
  1. "The 5-minute habit that fixed my focus" (benefit)
  2. "Why your to-do list is lying to you" (curiosity)
  3. "3 tools, 1 workflow, zero chaos" (specificity)
Preview text: "Plus: the calendar trick nobody talks about."

Intro: Quick one this week — three things that actually changed how I work.

🧠 Deep work
  **The focus protocol** — A simple time-boxing method; what's new is the
  2-min "shutdown ritual" — I tried it for a week and inbox-anxiety dropped. [link]
🛠️ Tools
  **Tool X** — Finally a calendar that... [your take]. [link]

→ CTA: Hit reply and tell me your #1 focus killer — I'll feature answers next week.
```

## Workflow

1. **Gather the raw material** (links/notes) and the issue's angle/theme.
2. **Curate ruthlessly** — keep only items worth the reader's time; group into sections.
3. **Write value-added summaries** (`summarize` for digestion + your take); front-load each.
4. **Craft 3–5 subject-line options + preview text**; write the intro hook.
5. **Set one primary CTA**; format for scannability and mobile.
6. **Apply the editorial voice** consistently (→ `brand-voice`); deliver the issue or the requested piece. Cross-promote across formats with `video-scriptwriter`/`youtube-content`.

## Key pitfalls

- **Weak/clickbait subject line.** Too vague gets ignored; misleading gets unsubscribes and spam flags. Specific + honest curiosity wins.
- **Wasted preview text.** Defaulting to "View in browser" throws away prime real estate — write it.
- **Echoing the source.** Copy-pasted blurbs add nothing; your *take* is the value.
- **Too many items / no curation.** Quantity dilutes; a few great picks beat a link dump.
- **No clear CTA (or too many).** One primary ask; don't make the reader guess.
- **Wall of text / not mobile-friendly.** Skimmable hierarchy, short paragraphs, phone-first.
- **Inconsistent voice/structure.** Regulars expect familiarity — keep voice and layout consistent.
- **Spam-trigger formatting** hurting deliverability — avoid CAPS/!!!/“FREE”.

## Quick reference

- Two make-or-break moments: subject line (the open) + first screen (the read).
- Subject ~30–50 chars, specific+curious not clickbait; offer 3–5 angles; write the preview text.
- Each item: headline + 2–4 sentence summary **with your take** + link. Curate ruthlessly.
- One primary CTA. Scannable (bold heads, short paragraphs, bullets), mobile-first, consistent structure.
- Avoid spam triggers; authentic from-name; A/B subjects via ab-testing.
- Digest sources with summarize; keep voice via brand-voice; repurpose via video-scriptwriter/youtube-content.
